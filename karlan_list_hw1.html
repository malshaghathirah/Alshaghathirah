A Replication of Karlan and List (2007)

Author: Mohammed Alshaghathirah
Date: April 2025

Introduction

Dean Karlan at Yale and John List at the University of Chicago conducted a field experiment to test the effectiveness of different fundraising letters. They sent out 50,000 fundraising letters to potential donors, randomly assigning each letter to one of several treatments: a standard letter (control), a matching grant letter, or a challenge grant letter.

This project seeks to replicate their results using the original dataset, focusing on whether offering a matching grant increases the probability of donation.

Data

Description

The dataset includes observations on individuals who received fundraising letters. Each individual was randomly assigned to a group. Some were offered a matching donation, while others were not.

We examine key variables:

  • treatment: 1 if the individual received a match offer
  • gave: 1 if the individual donated
  • amount: amount donated

We load and inspect the dataset below.

Balance Test

As a test of the randomization process, we compare selected covariates across the treatment and control groups. A well-randomized experiment should not show significant differences between the groups in non-outcome variables like prior donation history or demographics.

The t-test compares the average number of months since last donation (mrm2) between treatment and control groups. The regression models mrm2 as a function of treatment status.

If both the t-test and regression show a small, statistically insignificant difference (e.g., p-value > 0.05), we conclude that the groups are well balanced on this variable, meaning that randomization was successful. This aligns with what we expect from a properly randomized experiment.

Table 1 in the original paper serves the same purposeit shows that prior characteristics are not systematically different across groups, which builds confidence in the causal interpretation of treatment effects.

This suggests that the treatment primarily affects the extensive margin (i.e., whether someone donates) more than the intensive margin (i.e., how much they donate).

Simulation Experiment

To illustrate the Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem, we simulate donation data under the assumed true probabilities of giving:

  • Control group: 1.8% donation rate
  • Treatment group: 2.2% donation rate

We compare these simulated results to the theoretical expectations.

In[21]:
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt


np.random.seed(42)


control_sim = np.random.binomial(n=1, p=0.018, size=100000)
treatment_sim = np.random.binomial(n=1, p=0.022, size=100000)


differences = treatment_sim - control_sim


cum_avg_diff = np.cumsum(differences) / np.arange(1, len(differences)+1)


plt.figure(figsize=(10,5))
plt.plot(cum_avg_diff)
plt.axhline(y=0.004, color='red', linestyle='--', label='True Difference (0.022 - 0.018)')
plt.title("LLN Simulation: Cumulative Average of Difference in Giving")
plt.xlabel("Simulation Iteration")
plt.ylabel("Cumulative Average Difference")
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()

Law of Large Numbers

The cumulative average difference between treatment and control stabilizes around 0.004 as the number of trials increases. This confirms the Law of Large Numbers: with enough observations, sample averages converge to their expected values.

sample_sizes = [50, 200, 500, 1000]

plt.figure(figsize=(12, 10))

for i, n in enumerate(sample_sizes):
    sample_diffs = []
    for _ in range(1000):

        treatment = np.random.binomial(1, 0.022, n)

Central Limit Theorem

As the sample size increases, the distribution of mean differences between treatment and control becomes more concentrated and symmetric. Even though the underlying variable is binary, the sampling distribution of the mean difference approaches a normal shapethis demonstrates the Central Limit Theorem in action.

Conclusion

This replication of Karlan and List (2007) confirms several key findings from their influential field experiment on charitable giving:

  • Individuals who received a matching donation offer were significantly more likely to donate, as shown through both descriptive statistics and a Probit regression.
  • Larger match ratios (2:1 and 3:1) did not significantly increase donation rates beyond the standard 1:1 match, supporting the papers claim that higher match sizes do not necessarily lead to higher effectiveness.
  • While treatment increased the likelihood of giving, it did not have a statistically meaningful effect on the amount donatedindicating that matching offers influence the extensive margin (whether to donate), not the intensive margin (how much to donate).
  • Simulation exercises demonstrated the Law of Large Numbers and Central Limit Theorem, reinforcing the statistical validity of the results observed in the field experiment.

Overall, the analysis affirms the original authors conclusion that matching gifts are a useful strategy for increasing donor participation, but that the size of the match may not matter beyond a certain point. These insights are valuable for non-profits, economists, and behavioral researchers alike.
